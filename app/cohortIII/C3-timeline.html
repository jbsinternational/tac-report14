  • January 2014

    • East Carolina University

      January 16-17, 2014

      East Carolina University’s (ECU) College of Allied Health Sciences’ Department of Addictions and Rehabilitation Studies launched the Operation Reentry North Carolina: Veteran Resiliency and Reintegration Through Technology (ORNC: R&R) project to work with military veterans and their families living in rural, eastern North Carolina who are struggling with substance abuse and/or psychosocial challenges, many of whom are homeless and severely economically disadvantaged. ORNC: R&R staff deliver services to veterans using a mobile outreachvehicle equipped with state-of-the-art technology, including satellite communication, Web- and mobile-based applications, shortwave radio, GPS tracking, and Polycom teleconferencing capacity.

    • San Antonio Council on Alcohol and Drug Abuse

      January 9-10, 2014

      The San Antonio Council on Alcohol and Drug Abuse (SACADA) is a nonprofit agency that provides education, youth prevention programs, and alcohol and drug abuse prevention resources and services to Bexar County, a minority-majority populated county, and 29 surrounding counties in South Central Texas. SACADA’s Web-Oriented Recovery Care (known as WORC) project supports individuals seeking substance abuse treatment and recovery services through peer recovery coaches, culturally competent approaches, and an interactive Web site (www.recoverytexas.org) that is available in four languages and features valuable resources, self-evaluation instruments, and a chat function for clients. The program helps expand and enhance services to rural communities by overcoming barriers to transportation, language, and a lack of insurance and resources.

    • Operation PAR, Inc.

      January 30-31, 2014

      Operation Parental Awareness and Responsibility (Operation PAR), headquartered in Largo, Florida, is an incorporated nonprofit organization that addresses the substance abuse and mental health needs of those living in the Tampa Bay region. Operation PAR’s Technology-Assisted Care (TAC) in Pasco County project provides comprehensive substance abuse treatment services to those engaged in outpatient care. By incorporating tablets, smartphones, and Web-based technology, Operation PAR enhances existing treatment options and helps to increase service capacity among the area’s primary care and behavioral health providers. The program focuses on expanding services and improving health for pregnant and parenting women and will eventually serve older adults (55 and older) and veterans.

  • February 2014

    • Centerstone of Tennessee

      February 6-7, 2014

      Centerstone of Tennessee launched the Tennessee Web-Based Recovery-Oriented System of Care (TN e-ROSC) project to facilitate traditional treatment approaches through technology. The program uses a technology-enhanced version of the Recovery-Oriented System of Care (ROSC) model to improve client care coordination, communication, and health outcomes. The program makes use of several technology platforms to facilitate service delivery to clients, including a mobile-responsive and interactive Web site, text messaging, a smartphone mobile application, expanded telehealth services, and a client portal linked to their electronic health record system. The TN e-ROSC program will focus on delivering care to clients in nine underserved counties in middle Tennessee and three neighboring counties in Kentucky. Its expansive reach necessitates the use of technology-enhanced services to effectively and efficiently reduce health disparities and barriers to care.

    • Centers Inc.

      February 10-11, 2014

      The Centers, Inc., is a Joint Commission–accredited agency providing a broad continuum of care, delivering behavioral health services that help underserved individuals reach their highest level of functioning. The program provides services to clients of all ages struggling with substance abuse and co-occurring mental health disorders living in rural Marion and Citrus counties. To overcome transportation barriers and other obstacles to receiving care, The Centers offers patients telehealth services and mobile applications to support their recovery journey. The services also help close the gap in health disparities caused by poverty and lack of insurance.

  • March 2014

    • Native American Health Center Inc.

      March 10-11, 2014

      The Native American Health Center (NAHC) is a nonprofit agency serving the California Bay Area’s urban American Indian/Alaska Native population and other underserved groups in the vicinity. NAHC acts as both a health center offering comprehensive behavioral and mental health services and a community center for surrounding neighborhoods. NAHC’s iNative project offers clients culturally competent treatment, prevention, traditional healing services, and peer support to individuals with substance abuse and co-occurring mental health disorders. NAHC currently offers Web-based videoconferencing support and an onsite computer lab and is in the process of building an electronic health record platform with a patient portal.

    • Fountain House

      March 27-28, 2014

      Based in New York City, Fountain House launched the Electronic Application for Continuous Care and Expanded Sobriety Support (E-ACCESS) to supplement in-person recovery support for individuals with serious mental illness and co-occurring substance use disorders by offering e-technology recovery-oriented services. E-ACCESS uses a customized mobile application developed specifically for Fountain House members that offers enhanced access to recovery support tools (e.g., print and video resources, a meeting locator, a sobriety calculator, motivational quotes, a text hotline). The E-ACCESS program achieves the best possible engagement of high-risk, hard-to-reach clients in clubhouse services and improves outcomes among marginalized populations whose socioeconomic status and mental health issues would otherwise inhibit opportunities to receive care.

  • April 2014

    • Boston University

      April 14-15, 2014

      Boston University’s Center for Addictions Research and Services launched the Smartphone Technology To Reduce Relapse Among Latinos With Mental Health and Substance Abuse Disorders project to expand long-term recovery care options for Latinos with substance use and co-occurring mental health disorders. Latino/a adults who have completed residential treatment at partner site Casa Esperanza (Hope House) receive an Android smartphone equipped with the University of Wisconsin–designed Addiction Comprehensive Health Enhancement Support System (A-CHESS) mobile application system. The tool was adapted for Spanish-speaking clients (and referred to as Casa Esperanza Comprehensive Health Enhancement Support System––CASA CHESS) to assist with their recovery over the course of their year-long program participation.

    • Prestera Center for Mental Health Services, Inc

      April 24-25, 2014

      Prestera Center for Mental Health Services, Inc., is West Virginia’s largest nonprofit, community-based organization providing behavioral health care services to nine counties in West Virginia. The organization provides access to approximately 61 sites throughout the State, with a comprehensive list of services that offer clients a fully integrated system tailored to individual needs. Prestera’s Technology-Assisted Care project uses videoconferencing technology, an electronic health records platform, and two patient portals (MyHealth and OneHealth) to help enhance recovery and prevention services for underserved and economically disadvantaged clients with substance abuse and co-occurring mental health disorders.

  • May 2014

    • Community Health Center Inc.

      May 21-22, 2014

      Community Health Center, Inc. (CHCI), is a nonprofit, federally qualified health center and one of Connecticut’s leading providers of primary care, behavioral health, dental care, and social services. CHCI provides services to individuals with an elevated risk for opioid abuse, the uninsured, and underinsured and special populations living in low-income and high-crime metropolitan areas. Operating as a patient-centered medical home, CHCI manages 13 primary care sites statewide to help increase access to affordable care. CHCI’s Technology-Enhanced Access to Coordinated Healthcare and Buprenorphine Maintenance Therapy (TEACH BMT) project uses the technology-centered Extension for Community Healthcare Outcomes (known as ECHO) model to build a knowledge base among providers to deliver specialty services. The project uses telehealth to deliver and engage patients and improve care coordination.

    • Randolph County Caring Community, Inc.

      May 29-30, 2014

      Randolph County Caring Community Partnership, Inc. (RCCCP), launched the Client Access and Services Exchange (CASE) project to recruit and engage adults (18 or older) who are uninsured and underinsured living in central Missouri’s Randolph, Saline, Pettis, Lafayette, Boone, and Cooper counties in pretreatment, treatment, and recovery services. CASE has leveraged technologies developed by neighboring TCE-TAC grantee programs—First Call Alcohol/Drug Prevention and Recovery and Preferred Family HealthCare—to enhance information sharing and communication among clients, providers, and other agencies via a client portal called Mobile CASE, a certified electronic health record known as Community Connection Missouri (or CCMo), and a virtual 3-D environment.

  • June 2014

    • Meta House, Inc.

      June 10-11, 2014

      Meta House, located in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, launched the Healthy Connections project to help women manage their health and regain a substance-free lifestyle. Healthy Connections uses an electronic health record system and smartphone mobile applications to address the trauma-centered, gender-specific, and family-focused treatment needs of Milwaukee’s underserved women and their children who are living with co-occurring substance use and mental health disorders. By integrating technology into care, Healthy Connections improves client engagement and reduces traditional barriers to service delivery.

    • Heartview Foundation

      June 26-27, 2014

      The Heartview Foundation is a Joint Commission–accredited, nonprofit substance abuse treatment and education provider located in Bismarck, North Dakota. Its Recovery Network for Rural and Underserved project uses technology (e.g., smartphones, tablets, a Web-based evaluation platform, an electronic health record system, social networking sites, text messaging, email) to provide treatment and recovery support for individuals (aged 18 or older), including those with special needs related to ethnicity, opioid addiction, and co-occurring mental health disorders. The use of technology helps to reduce barriers to seeking treatment and recovery support that result from lack of transportation, rural isolation, and more recently, the increase of risk factors from the transient population related to the Bakken oil boom.
