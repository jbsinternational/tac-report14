Program Vision and Design

Success Factors

  • Many grantees have invested in technologies that support efficient and coordinated clinical service delivery for clients, such as Web-based client portals, text messaging, videoconferencing, and mobile applications.
  • The use of Web-based client portals and electronic health records helps participating agencies manage client needs more effectively and increase collaboration around treatment plans.
  • Many programs offer client-centered care to engage and retain clients in recovery services.
  • Technology enables clients to connect to recovery support services (such as discussion boards) anonymously and at times that work best for them.
  • Integrated electronic health records will enhance the ability of program staff to bill third-party vendors, collect patient demographics, schedule client appointments, and access data for evaluation purposes.
  • Technology is increasingly more accessible to clients and available in a variety of platforms. Some program staff have seen an increased uptake in technology use among younger clients
  • Technology makes it possible to reach clients, partnering agencies, and other stakeholders in ways that were once only possible by traveling long distances. This benefit allows for more frequent communication and increased visibility to foster engagement and participation.

Challenges

  • Some program staff have concerns about ensuring patient confidentiality and the cost of using certain technologies.
  • Integration of electronic health records is complicated when multiple platforms in use are not compatible with one another. In some cases, information is not transferrable across systems. Other challenges include accessibility of client data and certification of electronic health record systems.
  • Client use of technology can be inhibited by four factors: (1) cost (many live below the Federal poverty level), (2) inconsistent living arrangements, (3) unreliable access to technology (particularly smartphones with data plans), and (4) lack of support from family or peers who might also be dealing with substance abuse or co-occurring disorders.
  • Among rural populations, there can be a lack of understanding about mental health and substance use treatment. Specific groups (such as mentally ill and/or drug-using parents, cultural minorities, new immigrants, non-English speakers, and Native Americans) may be fearful of participating in government services.
  • Long-term client engagement and retention in treatment can be challenging. Program staff must effectively demonstrate the technology’s value to clients to encourage staying in care.

Grantee Leadership

Success Factors

  • Technology enables leadership and program staff to collaborate effectively to advance organizational goals.
  • When leadership staff believe in technology’s ability to enhance client outcomes, they can see the benefits to the organization as a whole.
  • People in leadership roles often have backgrounds in behavioral health care and understand the needs of their clients in ways that support program development efforts.
  • When people in leadership roles are well connected in their communities, they can leverage those connections to facilitate external support for program expansion.
  • People in leadership roles in the programs are often passionate, dedicated, and forward thinking.

Challenges

  • Partner agencies sometimes do not have technology champions at each active site. In many cases, administrators are reluctant to use technology for fear of disturbing workflow practices and concerns over client confidentiality.
  • Individuals in leadership roles may have limited understanding of the implications for integrating technology into practice as outlined by the Affordable Care Act.
  • Some program staff may view the use of technology as the purview of the IT department rather than as a clinical tool and a means to improve quality care and continuity of services.

Implementation

Success Factors

  • Provider agencies offer traditional services and an assortment of distance treatment modalities to enhance client recovery efforts. For example, they use electronic health records, client portals, Web sites, email, smartphone applications, text messaging, and videoconferencing.
  • Telehealth enables clients to access technology in a variety of ways from their personal computers, smartphones, and tablets.
  • In general, program staff have remained flexible in their implementation strategy and are willing to adapt delivery mechanisms as needed.
  • In some cases, additional social media strategies have been implemented to further expand outreach potential.
  • Technology can be used to support broader life skill development in areas such as employment, dealing with emotions, child welfare, and housing.

Challenges

  • Grantees may have limited staff resources to fully implement programs and manage expansion efforts. Some programs may not be able to support growth.
  • Staff time may be spread across several programs; therefore, maintaining a consistent focus is challenging, especially as programs expand and client enrollment increases.
  • Some technologies are less convenient to use, partly because of unreliable Internet connections in many rural areas. Other technologies are not as user friendly as anticipated. Clients can be less inclined to troubleshoot issues or remain engaged when technology does not perform as expected.
  • Integration of technology is influenced by staff workflow within provider agencies. Adoption of new policies and procedures must be consistent with the site’s culture and approach toward providing recovery services to clients. In some cases, this has meant incorporating technology more slowly than anticipated.
  • Providers who prefer traditional face-to-face client encounters can be less inclined to incorporate technology into their practice. Such reluctance can delay implementation.
  • Many providers are concerned that telehealth options infringe on patient confidentiality, even though the technologies are in wide use nationwide.
  • Clients may demonstrate some reluctance managing Web-based aspects of the treatment process on their own. A lack of Web experience, concerns about confidentiality, and a dearth of provider champions could inhibit use.
  • Integrating technology such as electronic health records into provider practices requires sufficient training and technical assistance support from system vendors. Time, resources, and willingness to troubleshoot technical issues are often limited in busy practice settings.
  • A high rate of staff turnover at partner agencies can hinder the ability to institutionalize new practices. Much time is required to train new staff and refine workflow.
  • Some partner agencies have opted not to participate in a program, creating a significant setback to achieving implementation goals.

Community Linkages, Partners, and Participation

Success Factors

  • A program’s leadership and staff can have strong ties to the communities where they operate. Many have active boards with established relationships with key stakeholders.
  • Many programs are actively partnering with community agencies to expand client services.
  • Many programs are working to diversify their workforce and streamline processes for those who wish to deliver treatment and recovery-oriented services to clients.

Challenges

  • A high rate of staff turnover at partner agencies can make it difficult to recruit and retain excellent health care providers whose support can help institutionalize a program beyond the grant’s life.
  • In some cases, linkages with other organizations could be further explored and strengthened.
  • Several sites that were initially approached to participate in the program determined they did not have the clients or the interest in incorporating technology into their recovery curriculum.
  • Program implementation can be taxed by geographical distance between sites. Technology is essential to effectively manage the program among participating agencies.
  • In some cases, leadership may benefit from more sustained connections with the appropriate State agencies and community organizations regarding policymaking and decisionmaking related to health information technology and the Affordable Care Act.

Client Outreach, Recruitment, and Referral

Success Factors

  • Many programs are actively recruiting new community partners to increase client referral opportunities. Targeted outreach to individuals and organizations in a variety of different settings—court systems, health clinics, military installations, behavioral health centers, and schools, to name a few—will help expand service potential.
  • In many cases, program staff are using a variety of marketing strategies—such as radio, print, and television announcements—to promote their activities. They are also escalating online marketing capacity by using social media platforms such as Facebook, YouTube, Twitter, and LinkedIn to reach clients and raise community awareness.
  • Many programs have effectively used Web sites and developed tools such as videos and Webinars to create an accessible and convenient user experience for staff and clients.

Challenges

  • Some programs are unable to support expansion efforts because of workflow limitations or provider reluctance to incorporate technology into their practice settings.
  • A high rate of staff turnover at partner agencies can contribute to recruitment and retention challenges.
  • Identifying program champions and mentors among agency staff has been difficult. Candidates often do not know enough about the technology to see the value of using it with their clients.
  • Several programs do not have a marketing plan in place. An effective plan should outline client outreach strategies, suggest relationship-building opportunities, and include a multiplatform approach to target stakeholders, using both traditional and electronic marketing mediums.

Affordable Care Act Readiness

Success Factors

  • Many programs are well positioned to meet the requirements of the Affordable Care Act (ACA).
  • Often individuals in leadership roles are engaged in State and local policy development activities to prepare for agency-wide changes.
  • Some programs have established partnerships with primary care providers and are actively working to integrate their systems to support continuity of care for clients.
  • Several programs are using electronic health records (EHRs) that have been certified by the Certification Commission for Health Information Technology.
  • Many programs are actively planning to achieve Stage 2 Meaningful Use goals.
  • Many grantees are strongly committed to using health information technology and other strategies to enhance technology-assisted care.

Challenges

  • Provider agencies are at different stages of implementing EHR systems. Some have not implemented a system in their practice, while others are using different systems that are not compatible with one another. This can hinder communications and limit the ability to provide the best possible support for patients.
  • Modifying existing EHRs to comply with ACA requirements can be cost prohibitive.
  • Payment structures may not provide incentives for using technology. Current provider reimbursement schedules are based on the number of clients staff see (i.e., billable activities), and using technology takes away from billable hours. Payment schedules will radically shift under ACA, which will focus on reimbursement for patient outcomes, not individual service events.

Sustainability

Success Factors

  • Many programs understand the value of distance treatment technology related to client outcomes and appreciate the role technology will have on the future of patient care. While implementation has been the primary focus, staff are generally mindful of the importance of sustaining technology beyond the grant’s life.
  • Using technology is a means to make capacity-building efforts more efficient. Dollars typically set aside for building development can be diverted to expand technology to a wider target population.

Challenges

  • Third-party billing systems (Medicaid, Medicare, and private insurance) are important drivers of program sustainability. Many programs do not have payment structures in place to reimburse for the use of technology.
  • Some programs lack a robust sustainability plan that addresses program design, workflow, and reimbursement, among other critical components. Integrating care across systems and engaging in health information exchanges will be essential to program survival.

Evaluation

Success Factors

  • Some evaluation and data collection efforts are much more inclusive than what is required to meet Government Performance and Results Act (GPRA) requirements. Programs have incorporated existing instruments or designed their own to assess technology’s role in enhancing client outcomes.
  • Using gift cards and other incentives are effective strategies to boost client GPRA followup rates.
  • Extracting data from client portals and using tracking analytics enhance the ability to measure the effectiveness of service delivery mechanisms.
  • A comprehensive program evaluation plan and dedicated evaluation staff are necessary to produce robust findings.
  • System interoperability expands data collection opportunities.

Challenges

  • Obtaining followup data from transient or precariously housed individuals can be difficult. Programs are encouraged to acquire multiple points of contact.
  • GPRA data do not directly measure the effects of technology on client outcomes. Programs must use other assessment tools to determine the extent to which technology is making a difference.
  • Data collection is time consuming and labor intensive. Partner agencies are reluctant to divert staff resources toward this effort, particularly because of workforce and budget constraints.
  • Client data can become isolated when it is not possible to communicate across EHR systems. A lack of access to site analytics represents a missed opportunity to inform evaluation efforts.
