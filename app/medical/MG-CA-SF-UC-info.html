Goals

  • Enhance SBIRT teaching and practice in all UCSF primary care residencies.
  • Determine the effectiveness of the SBIRT curriculum and subsequent changes in clinical care and/or systems-based practice.
  • Ensure the sustainability and continuation of the SBIRT curriculum and improvements in clinical care.
  • Disseminate the SBIRT curriculum, evaluation tools, and lessons learned to the broader medical and educational communities.

Population Served

  • The target population is the ethnically diverse, urban residents of the city and county of San Francisco (population 780,000).
  • UCPC residents see patients at 2 urban clinics (Parnassus and Mount Zion) with more than 55,000 visits per year. The patients served reflect the ethnic and economic diversity of San Francisco: approximately 9 percent are Latino, 12 percent are African American, 22 percent are Asian/Pacific Islander, 46 percent are White, and 11 percent are “other”.
  • Insurance coverage is also diverse: 57 percent of patients have commercial contracted care, 28 percent have Medicare, 11 percent have MediCal (i.e., Medicaid), and 3 percent are self-pay.
  • The Parnassus clinic mostly serves medically disabled, elderly, working poor, and middle-class patients with serious medical comorbidities.
  • The Mount Zion clinic, located between robust African American and Asian communities, serves lower to middle-income patients, including large groups of African American and Asian American older adults.
  • UCPC residents see a range of adult patients, with high proportions of older adults and patients with complex medical comorbidities. More than 50 percent of clinic visits are with patients 55 or older.

Trainee Populations

  • Year 1 focus: General internal medicine primary care faculty and residents
  • Year 2 focus: Categorical faculty and residents (individuals with subspecializations in internal medicine)
  • Years 3–5 focus: Faculty and residents in specialties including pediatrics, emergency medicine, OB/GYN, and psychiatry
  • Psychologists; social workers; nurse practitioners; and nursing, pharmacy, and medical students
