Partnering Organizations

  • Vermont Department of Health, Division of Alcohol and Drug Abuse Programs
  • SBIRT Training Institute, hosted by the University of Vermont (UVM), College of Medicine

Goals

  • Increase access to universal screening, secondary prevention, early intervention, and treatment for people engaging in substance misuse or abuse by implementing SBIRT in primary care and other health settings.
  • Develop a systematic training model that efficiently and effectively promotes needed clinical skills, practice competency, and fidelity in SBIRT evidence-based practices to a broad range of health care providers through Webinars, courses, onsite coaching/feedback, and clinical toolbox resources.
  • Ensure a sustainable Vermont-SBIRT model within Vermont’s health care system.
  • Develop the health information technology infrastructure to facilitate the use of SBIRT and enhance communication among physical health care and behavioral health care providers.

Implementation Sites

  • Federally qualified health centers (FQHCs)
  • Vermont Coalition of Clinics for the Uninsured
  • Vermont National Guard’s medical center
  • UVM’s student health center
  • Central Vermont Medical Center/Satellite primary care clinics

Population Served

  • Among young adults aged 18–25, national data indicate Vermont is among the top five States with the highest past-month prevalence of alcohol use (74 percent), binge drinking (51 percent), marijuana use (33 percent), and illicit drug use other than marijuana (11 percent).
  • The primary target group is low-income, racially/ethnically diverse adults with limited formal education. These individuals are less likely to access behavioral health services, and they represent a large percentage of those served.
  • The secondary target group is young adults aged 18–25. This cohort has a high rate of misuse of alcohol and other drugs and has the highest unmet need for receiving treatment.
  • Among individuals served by the FQHCs and free clinics, approximately one-half to two-thirds receive Medicaid/Medicare, and the majority of sites report more than 18 percent of their patients as uninsured. Between 55 percent and 100 percent of the FQHC and clinic patients are living below the national poverty level.
  • In one of the largest participating FQHCs—Burlington’s Community Health Center—11 percent of the patients are homeless, 28 percent identify as belonging to a culturally diverse race, and 11 percent are best served in a language other than English.
  • UVM’s student health center serves two-thirds of the school’s 10,000 students.
