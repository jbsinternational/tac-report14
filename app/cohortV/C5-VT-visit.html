Program Vision and Design

Strengths

  • Vermont endorses a broad public health approach to substance use and views SBIRT as an essential set of services within primary care.
  • The State endorses and supports behavioral health/primary care integration.

Challenges

  • The State appears to be identifying and promoting a model of integrated care that aligns with the unique systems and context of the State.

Grantee Leadership

Strengths

  • The SBIRT initiative receives high-level leadership support within Vermont Department of Health.
  • The project management team is knowledgeable of SBIRT and implementation of evidence-based programs.
  • Vermont-SBIRT has developed medical professional champions who publicly support SBIRT.
  • The State’s policy steering committee includes high-level representation from Medicaid, private insurance, the health information exchange, and primary care and behavioral health.

Challenges

  • State purchasing processes are lengthy and resulted in startup delays requiring corrective action to meet grant performance targets.
  • Nursing staff appear to need further education and support to build their knowledge base and support for SBIRT adoption.

Implementation Plan

Strengths

  • The grantee’s implementation approach was designed to achieve the greatest likelihood of program sustainability.
  • Vermont-SBIRT individualizes implementation workflow across practice sites.
  • Comprehensive training and coaching are provided to onsite staff to build core SBIRT skills.
  • The grantee has developed and piloted a specific approach to screening adult marijuana use that reduces the number of false-positive prescreens and appears better aligned to the use patterns of the adult population.
  • Vermont builds on nearly 10 years of dedicated effort in health information technology. Most primary care systems are currently engaged with the State’s health information exchange, and the State behavioral health system is preparing for inclusion.

Challenges

  • Vermont-SBIRT was delayed in its startup.
  • Several sites are working through issues related to workflow and adoption of SBIRT that are common in startup.
  • The State behavioral health systems have lagged behind primary care in adoption of electronic health records.
  • Questions regarding SBIRT and other substance use-related patient data sharing are still being negotiated between the single State agency and the State health information exchange.

Community Linkages, Partners, and Participation

Strengths

  • Vermont has successfully engaged with essential stakeholders statewide in support of the SBIRT initiative.

Challenges

  • None noted.

Client Outreach, Recruitment, and Referral

Strengths

  • Referral relationships are in place between all primary care providers and the State-preferred provider network.

Challenges

  • There is a long wait-list for services for more intensive levels of care such as residential treatment.

Affordable Care Act Readiness

Strength

  • The State of Vermont has been a national leader in adoption of health information technology and the implementation of the Nation’s first State single-payer health insurance system.

Challenge

  • Behavioral health/primary care integration is a goal of the State. Efforts have begun, but defined models remain a work in progress.

Sustainability Planning

Strength

  • The State has implemented its grant in ways that support long-term SBIRT implementation beyond grant funding.
  • The State has engaged essential stakeholders on its policy steering committee to address policy and payer issues that affect the development of a viable and sustainable business model.

Challenge

  • Elements of the current SBIRT insurance policies may create challenges for providers developing viable business practice models.

Grantee Evaluation

Strength

  • Vermont has engaged an experienced evaluation group that is closely involved with formative and outcome evaluation activities.
  • The evaluator has created an innovative “data dashboard” that graphically represents performance data that are easily understandable to broad constituencies.

Challenge

  • Certain practice sites are employing highly trained staff to collect Government Performance and Results Act (GPRA) data. This practice has often proven challenging in other settings.

Recommended Enhancements

GRANTEE-LED ENHANCEMENTS

  • Program Vision and Design
    • Undertake efforts to identify and describe existing models of integrated care and their key components that would fit within Vermont.
    • Identify and build buy-in among key stakeholders necessary to effect systems change.
  • Grantee Leadership
    • Develop a plan to expand service capacity to meet agreed-upon performance targets.
    • Groom nursing staff as practice champions.
  • Implementation Plan
    • Closely monitor performance and increase the number of providers to meet performance targets.
    • Continue providing support and technical assistance to practice sites working through startup issues.
    • Document findings regarding SBIRT and adult marijuana use as the information may have broad benefit across systems.
    • Develop a written plan guiding SBIRT health information technology implementation and integration. (A planning template has been provided to Vermont-SBIRT, and the State may wish to seek technical assistance supporting the development of this health information technology strategic plan.)
  • Affordable Care Act Readiness
    • Build an understanding of integrated care models.
    • Look beyond broad thematic descriptions of integration to better understand and identify business practice models that would fit within existing State systems.
  • Sustainability Planning
    • Consider working collaboratively with payers and providers to identify and negotiate necessary payment for services.
  • Grantee Evaluation
    • Use fewer credentialed staff to conduct GRPA data collection activities.
