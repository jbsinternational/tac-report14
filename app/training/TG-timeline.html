  • February 2014

    • University of South Carolina

      February 5–6, 2014

      The InterDisciplinary Collaboration and Implementation of SBIRT Curricula in University of South Carolina Health Systems (IDISCUS) program plans to implement, adapt where necessary, and evaluate SAMHSA’s SBIRT curriculum in its health professional programs for social work, nursing, rehabilitation counseling, and four medical residency programs (internal medicine, preventive medicine, family medicine, and neuropsychiatry). The program anticipates training more than 600 students over 3 years. It seeks to educate students in both didactic and practice settings to implement and promote SBIRT as a strategy for reaching a large number of South Carolina residents at risk for substance use disorders. The program will also disseminate SBIRT training to 4,000 multidisciplinary health professionals throughout the State health care system.

  • March 2014

    • University of Colorado Denver

      March 20–21, 2014

      Nurses Helping Colorado aims to train bachelor’s-level nursing students and master’s-level Advanced Practice Registered Nurse (APRN) students to become part of a culturally competent workforce that will help integrate behavioral health and primary care and implement SBIRT as part of a multidisciplinary team. The program plans to train 825 nursing program graduates over 3 years, leading to 675 new RN-level nurses and 150 APRN independent practitioners trained to implement SBIRT. Situated in a large and integrated health care system, the program provides extensive preceptorship in real-world practice settings.

  • April 2014

    • City University of New York, Lehman College

      April 2–3, 2014

      This SBIRT program is training bachelor of arts and master of social work students and pediatric medical residents to perform SBIRT in health care and social service settings in impoverished, underserved communities in Bronx, New York, and surrounding urban areas. The program has made progress in implementing SBIRT training in the Department of Social Work: Faculty are actively involved, a core curriculum and faculty guide have been developed, faculty buy-in is strong, and nearly half of the social work field instructors have been trained. Training for medical residents is in the initial implementation stages: Preceptors have been trained, and residents have received some training. The council of directors is active and has met to discuss social work trainees’ practice of SBIRT in field placements, tracking and evaluating trainees’ use of SBIRT, and program sustainability. To encourage students and residents to use SBIRT after they complete training, the program’s community agency team will work toward cultivating relationships with field practice sites and preceptors.

    • Boston Children’s Hospital

      April 8–9, 2014

      The Training in Adolescent SBIRT for Medical Professionals program plans to train students and fellows—including nurses, social workers, and subspecialty physicians who care for adolescent patients—to extend their medical professional knowledge and workforce capacity for undertaking SBIRT effectively. The program also seeks to disseminate SBIRT throughout the Boston community and to establish partnerships with health care entities throughout the State. The program has made progress in implementing training at the various institutions and is offering SBRIT training to preceptors. Experienced core program staff have worked toward gaining commitments from various internal departments and external educational institutions throughout Boston. Leadership from the various entities determine where SBIRT best fits into their respective curricula and training programs. The leaders—represented on the council of directors—help ensure continued commitment after grant funding ends.

    • Indiana University

      April 8–9, 2014

      The Advancing Multidisciplinary Education for SBIRT program plans to customize, implement, and evaluate SAMHSA’s SBIRT curriculum in its health professional programs for social work, nursing, and medical residency. The program also plans to integrate the SBIRT curriculum in select courses across Indiana, create tailored Web-based educational modules, conduct face-to-face motivational interviewing training, and partner with behavioral health and primary care entities to diffuse SBIRT throughout the community. So far, the online curriculum modules and training materials are nearly complete. Implementation is planned to begin in the School of Social Work in June 2014 and in the School of Nursing and the College of Medicine in fall 2014. More than 50 sites are planned for student and medical resident internships and field placements.

    • Mercer University

      April 8–9, 2014

      The Southeast Consortium for Substance Abuse Training: Advanced Practice Registered Nurses (APRNs) SBIRT program plans to train about 1,500 master’s-level APRN students plus faculty and preceptors at 8 universities in Alabama, Georgia, and Maryland. Each site has a coordinator responsible for integrating SBIRT into the school’s curriculum, coordinating faculty and preceptors, developing a plan to measure student performance, and serving on the council of directors. The program model offers a mix of standardized nurse-specific SBIRT content and a central management unit administering to several satellite institutions. To date, the program has trained all site coordinators, developed all 19 training modules, and is using excellent evaluation tools. At least two faculty per site are to be trained.

    • University of Alaska Anchorage

      April 15–16, 2014

      Arctic-SBIRT plans to adapt and integrate the SBIRT curriculum into existing courses within the schools of nursing, social work, and psychology and within the Alaska Family Medicine Residency program. The program plans to train approximately 320 students and residents per year over 3 years, striving for a curriculum that is relevant to students’ respective disciplines, rurally tailored, and culturally and linguistically appropriate. Staff are working toward establishing SBIRT practice opportunities within the university, institutional policies to sustain inclusion of the SBIRT curriculum as a graduation requirement, and resources and interdisciplinary training opportunities to expand the curriculum’s reach. To date, the program has pilot-tested the curriculum in four nursing courses, two psychology courses, and two social work courses; trained family medicine residents during their psychiatric rotations through didactics and skills-based learning using the university’s simulation center; and obtained interdisciplinary commitment from the various faculty members on the council of directors.

    • University of Missouri-Kansas City

      April 16–17, 2014

      The Transforming the Academic Preparation of Health Professionals: Competency-Based SBIRT Training program plans to train more than 1,700 faculty members, bachelor of science in nursing (B.S.N.) students, Advanced Practice Registered Nurse students, master of social work students, trainees in local/regional systems of care, and online continuing education unit trainees. B.S.N. and social work courses are taught in a traditional, face-to-face learning environment, while most graduate-level nursing courses are taught primarily online. All trainees engage in skills practice through role-play with “standardized patients.” The program will also advocate for SBIRT integration into clinical sites that offer practicum opportunities and partner with clinical practice sites at several major medical centers to disseminate SBIRT training and technical assistance.

    • University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee

      April 21–22, 2014

      The SBIRT Training for Substance Misuse Program at the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee will train multidisciplinary teams of master’s-level social workers, student nurse practitioners, and medical residents in the SBIRT model. The program will also train licensed clinical social workers and other licensed professionals such as nurse practitioners and physicians in SBIRT through continuing education and onsite trainings. The program will increase future medical system capacity by growing the number of students and licensed professionals outside the specialty alcohol and other drug abuse treatment systems who have been introduced to and can provide State-certified SBIRT services. The program anticipates attaining its year 1 training goals. It has many supportive partner organizations, an active council of directors, and a comprehensive evaluation plan.

    • The Catholic University of America

      April 24–25, 2014

      The university’s School of Social Service and School of Nursing will use a multidisciplinary approach, including field education and clinical experiences, to train 450 master’s-level social work and nursing students over 3 years. Students will work with clients of diverse racial and ethnic backgrounds; vulnerable and at-risk clients; and active-duty military, veterans, and their families. The program will provide faculty, preceptor, and field instructor orientation trainings plus continuing education events and multidisciplinary teamwork. Trainings will emphasize working with administrations and organizations to implement changes that include SBIRT in the standard of care.

  • May 2014

    • University of California, Los Angeles

      May 1–2, 2014

      The Los Angeles SBIRT Network (LA SBIRT) will develop, implement, and evaluate SBIRT training for health professional students from a variety of disciplines. Its strong executive committee provides training, SBIRT educational materials, evaluation, oversight, and technical assistance to the various departments. The program is working to create a network of stakeholders in the Los Angeles health care system and substance abuse treatment programs to accelerate the rate of engagement in treatment.

    • University of Miami

      May 6–7, 2014

      The university’s School of Nursing and Health Sciences trains nurses, nursing students, medical residents, social workers, and counselors. The program team includes two schools within the University of Miami (Education and Nursing); the School of Social Work from Florida International University; and diverse practice settings that reach poor, underserved, largely minority populations. The program plans to train approximately 450 students per year in the classroom and several teams of supervisory professionals and students in practice settings. It will provide at least one regional training event per year. The program has an active council of directors and a network of community relationships with a history of delivering culturally appropriate substance use interventions to Hispanic patients. So far, core program staff have been trained, and team members are providing training to students, faculty, clinical staff, and staff of partnering organizations.

  • June 2014

    • Allegheny-Singer Research Institute

      June 12–13, 2014

      The Pennsylvania SBIRT Interdisciplinary Training Program anticipates training more than 2,857 medical residents and medical, pharmacy, dental, nursing, and social work students over 3 years. The program plans to disseminate SBIRT throughout the health care community by establishing partnerships and training SBIRT champions within the local health network. The program has created a Web site as a host portal for the SBIRT curriculum, for faculty and student resources, and for reported data. Materials have been reviewed and adapted using core competencies for each discipline to ensure appropriateness for the allied health professionals and students being trained. The experienced core program staff have gained commitments from departments within the Allegheny Health Network whose leaders are represented on the council of directors and will help ensure continued commitment to the project after the grant ends.

    • University of Vermont

      June 26–27, 2014

      The Vermont SBIRT Collaborative: Changing Care Through Systems Redesign and Team-Based Training program aims to train graduate students from across several colleges and departments—medical and family residents, social workers, nurses, and counselors—over the 3 grant years. The program’s interdisciplinary approach includes online didactic skills training, workshops, and team-based training with standardized patients. Other plans include training lead faculty from each department to ensure sustainability, placing students and residents in practice settings, and delivering community SBIRT training at sites throughout Vermont. Leaders from the various departments determine where SBIRT best fits into their respective curricula and training programs. The leaders—represented on the council of directors—will help ensure continued commitment after grant funding ends.
