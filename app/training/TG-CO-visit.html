PROGRAM VISION AND DESIGN

Strengths

  • The focus on nursing has the potential to dramatically increase the reach of SBIRT by developing “SBIRT-ready” bachelor of science in nursing (B.S.N.) students who will be able to support systemic implementation when and where that happens and master’s-level Advanced Practice Registered Nurse (APRN) students who are destined for larger roles as primary care providers and agents of change in their clinical venues.
  • The elements of SBIRT are integrated into the full spectrum of courses throughout the B.S.N. curriculum and in key coursework in the APRN curriculum.

Challenges

  • While weaving SBIRT throughout the curriculum can be seen as a strength, it also may come with challenges, such as lack of a cohesive and holistic view of SBIRT.

GRANTEE LEADERSHIP

Strengths

  • The grantee has a strong and motivated group of leaders and champions within the University of Colorado College of Nurses (CU CON).
  • Including Brie Reimann and Leigh Fisher from SBIRT Colorado brings strong support.
  • The council of directors has been formed and is already expanding SBIRT in the University of Colorado Hospital (UCH).

Challenges

  • Most of council of directors members are part of CU CON or work at UCH.

IMPLEMENTATION PLAN

Strengths

  • Nurses Helping Colorado (NHC) has worked hard to promote SBIRT and get buy-in from faculty and staff within CU CON and at practice sites.
  • NHC has tailored the SBIRT core curriculum to be more relevant to nurses and has created nurse-focused role-play scenarios.

Challenges

  • NHC still finds students need more nurse-centered SBIRT materials.

COMMUNITY LINKAGES, PARTNERS, AND PARTICIPATION

Strengths

  • NHC has developed strong partnerships with most of the important service providers in the area.
  • NHC is part of an independent group that includes, among others, SBIRT Colorado and Dr. Paul Seale. This group is attempting to build a community of practice around SBIRT and nursing.

Challenges

  • The program is by nature nurse-centric. While nurses surely are an innovative and important group of providers to train, NHC must continue to take steps to avoid becoming insular.

EXPERIENTIAL LEARNING

Strengths

  • Sheridan Federally Qualified Health Center and Denver Health are models of experiential learning, and they have made SBIRT part of their routine service provision.
  • CU CON has created a strong practicum program.

Challenges

  • There is a lack of buy-in from preceptors.
  • Most clinical practice sites where students follow their courses of study are not implementing SBIRT routinely.

AFFORDABLE CARE ACT READINESS

Strengths

  • CU CON is preparing students to use electronic health records.

Challenges

  • The EPIC electronic health record system at UCH includes SBIRT questions, but most providers are not asking them.

SUSTAINABILITY PLANNING

Strengths

  • Embedding SBIRT content into all CU CON curricula should facilitate sustainability.
  • NHC is creating scenarios for role-plays and would like to create a workbook.

Challenges

  • The NHC Web site is not linked to the broader CU Web site.

GRANTEE EVALUATION

Strengths

  • NHC has created a strong conceptual framework for evaluating the program.
  • NHC is being thoughtful and deliberate in crafting its evaluation methodology.

Challenges

  • Knowledge, skills, and attitudes are not being captured.
  • Government Performance and Results Act (GPRA) data collection timeframes have presented a problem because content is woven through the B.S.N. program and integrated into selected APRN courses.

RECOMMENDED ENHANCEMENTS

Grantee-Led Enhancements

  • Program Vision and Design
    • Incorporate a focused introduction to SBIRT in the B.S.N. program, building on it in subsequent courses: This introduction is planned to take place in the middle or latter part of the curriculum once students have developed a solid understanding of the clinical environment.
  • Grantee Leadership
    • Consider diversifying membership on the council of directors with members from academic and clinical institutions who work in disciplines other than nursing.
  • Community Linkages, Partners, and Participation
    • Consider continuing to develop partnerships outside of nursing in both academic and clinical settings, such as faculty in the School of Medicine, medical directors in key clinical venues, system leadership, physicians, and behavioral health professionals.
  • Experiential Learning
    • Offer preceptors SBIRT training that includes continuing education units.
    • Advocate for more SBIRT implementation at the most popular practicum site—UCH.
    • Work with SBIRT Colorado and other practicum sites that routinely implement SBIRT to ensure SBIRT experiential opportunity and preceptor assessment.
  • Affordable Care Act Readiness
    • Encourage UCH to make providers accountable for implementing SBIRT by making it mandatory in EPIC, the electronic health record system.
  • Grantee Evaluation
    • Develop a method to evaluate student knowledge, skills, and attitudes. As planned, this evaluation may be a combination of tests and coding role-plays.
    • Collect GPRA followup data at the conclusion of students’ CU CON education, or include a focused SBIRT introduction within the first month and collect GPRA followup data at 30 days.

Need for Technical Assistance Requests

  • Implementation Plan
    • Create a series of nurse-centered SBIRT modules and videos.
  • Affordable Care Act Readiness
    • Create a mockup of the EPIC interface to be used in CU CON courses.
  • Sustainability Planning
    • Obtain information technology support to link the NHC Web site to the broader CU Web site.
    • Obtain assistance in creating nursing-centered SBIRT materials that will help sustain the program in case of large staff turnover.
