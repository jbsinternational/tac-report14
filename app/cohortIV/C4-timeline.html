  • May 2012

    • Indiana

      May 8–9, 2012

      The Indiana SBIRT Initiative is launching SBIRT in 10 Wishard community health centers and at least 1 Midtown community mental health center in Indianapolis. The project utilizes behavioral health specialists to deliver SBIRT services within clinic settings in urban, suburban, and rural jurisdictions.

    • Connecticut

      May 10–11, 2012

      The Connecticut SBIRT Program aims to implement services in 10 community health centers statewide. At least 85 percent of the population served in these clinics are Medicaid or Medicare recipients, and a high percentage of these patients are of racial and linguistic minorities. Connecticut SBIRT plans to eventually extend operations to sites serving National Guard members, Reserve members, and their families.

    • New York

      May 15–17, 2012

      NYSBIRT seeks to model a successful SBIRT program in two large, high-need, culturally and ethnically diverse but systemically different systems: New York City public STD clinics, and multiple hospitals/clinics targeting the veteran population in Jefferson County, where Fort Drum is located. NYSBIRT plans to disseminate these models across the State.

    • American Samoa

      May 21–25, 2012

      The O le Alofa SBIRT program is being implemented in two community health centers that serve patients with non-emergency primary health concerns. Each of these centers has a designated space for SBIRT and the support of the primary physician on staff. SBIRT is also being introduced at a public health clinic and a hospital Emergency Department.

  • June 2012

    • Illinois

      June 7–8, 2012

      Illinois is a returning SBIRT grant recipient, having been awarded a Cohort I State SBIRT grant in 2003. During the first award, SBIRT services were delivered in three general hospitals and three community-based health clinics. The Illinois SBIRT II project is focusing on offering services through federally qualified health centers in the metropolitan Chicago area. Included in the target population are adults involved with the criminal justice system receiving services at non-correctional community settings.

    • Tennessee

      June 12–13, 2012

      The State of Tennessee is introducing SBIRT into at least six health centers. Program recipients are primarily low-income patients in Appalachia and urban areas. Tennessee SBIRT intends to disseminate SBIRT statewide within primary care systems through infrastructure development, including training, technical assistance, and enhanced communication between primary care physicians and drug and alcohol providers. Tennessee is also piloting SBIRT implementation into the State National Guard.

    • North Carolina

      June 20–21, 2012

      NC SBIRT is imlementing SBIRT into highly diverse settings including a family practice clinic associated with a large teaching hospital and a true "free clinic" serving indigent and uninsured adults, many of whom are urban Black and Hispanic immigrants. Other sites include a suburban primary care practice, a rural health center, and a center serving a large Native American population. New staff positions, "Care Ambassadors," and Licensed Clinical Social Worker/Licensed Clinical Addiction Specialists (LCSW/LCASs) assist patients with the SBIRT process and with access to services.

    • Washington

      June 20–21, 2012

      The WASBIRT-PCI project aims to implement SBIRT services for adults receiving primary care in community health clinics in King County. WASBIRT-PCI has five ultimate goals for implementation, based on findings from the previous WASBIRT grant: 1) Reduced alcohol and drug use, 2) Increased entrance to chemical dependency treatment, 3) Reduced medical costs, 4) Reduced criminal justice involvement, and 5) Reduced mortality.

  • August 2012

    • Colorado

      August 13–14, 2012

      Building on its success as a Cohort II grantee, Colorado SBIRT plans to further disseminate SBIRT to primary care and community settings that have not yet been reached and to institutionalize the Colorado SBIRT model as a standard of care. The population served is composed primarily of patients at rural and urban health centers and a public dental clinic.

  • May 2013

    • Iowa

      May 27–29, 2013

      The Iowa Department of Public Health is implementing SBIRT in federally qualified health centers in four regions with demonstrated community need consistent with prevalence data, incidence of alcohol-related illness and poverty, and potential impact. In addition to successfully implementing SBIRT in primary care settings, Iowa has used evidence-based brief treatment and successfully adopted SBIRT within the Iowa Army National Guard, including the use of telehealth to serve soldiers in remote locations. Iowa SBIRT partners have demonstrated exemplary cooperation among State offices, primary care sites, behavioral health organizations, and the National Guard. The grantee has also developed manuals that support SBIRT implementation and GPRA followup.

  • June 2013

    • Arizona

      June 4–7, 2013

      The Arizona SBIRT program (AZ-SBIRT) aims to reduce substance-related mortality and the prevalence of substance use disorders in northern Arizona by expanding and enhancing the State’s system of behavioral health care to include SBIRT services. At the time of the site visit, five provider sites—including emergency departments and community health clinics—had begun implementing SBIRT at their facilities, with SBIRT services administered by existing staff. Grant funds had not been used to place personnel exclusively dedicated to implementing SBIRT at each site, and providers were administering only screenings and brief interventions and referring clients as needed to an outside entity to receive treatment. Several new provider sites were identified to implement SBIRT with their client populations in upcoming months, and services were scheduled to begin at Yavapai Regional Medical Center and Canyonlands Healthcare in July 2013 and at Kingman Regional Medical Center in January 2014. Medical providers and emergency department staff at all sites were to be trained on SBIRT protocols, and institutional policies were to be developed to prescreen all patients and adhere to the SBIRT process.

  • September 2013

    • New Jersey

      September 12–13, 2013

      New Jersey SBIRT faced four challenges that delayed startup and implementation: (1) Hurricane Sandy created a public health and safety crisis, diverted State resources and attention, and shut down transportation at a critical time; (2) The former Department of Addiction Services merged with the Department of Mental Health to form the Department of Mental Health and Addiction Services, and several key personnel left or assumed new positions; (3) A calculation error in the State’s winning proposal required reconfiguring patient numbers and practice sites; and (4) The principal subcontractor changed its corporate status, requiring the State to reissue an RFP, select Henry J. Austin Health Center, and work creatively to engage other participating agencies. Because of these startup delays, New Jersey was placed on “high risk” and required to provide SAMHSA with a corrective action plan. At the time of the site visit, New Jersey had not yet completed and submitted the plan.
