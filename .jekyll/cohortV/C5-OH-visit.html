Ohio

  • Program Information
  • Site Visit Report

Dates:
March 24–26, 2014

Site Visit Team Members:
Contracting Staff: Joseph Hyde, M.A., LMHC; Beth Johnson, M.P.H.; Dave Wanser, Ph.D.

Sites Visited:
Ohio Department of Mental Health & Addiction Services
Gene Wright Community Health Center
New Carlisle Community Health Center

Program Vision and Design

Strengths

  • The State views SBIRT as an essential part of an overarching strategy that supports behavioral health and primary care integration.
  • An integrated health information exchange (HIE) is part of the State’s vision.
  • The grantee has included statewide dissemination of SBIRT in its overall program design.

Challenges

  • Primary care and behavioral health have been historically separated with limited integration. This separation is perpetuated by beliefs and policies that create challenges to integration.

Grantee Leadership

Strengths

  • Senior leadership strongly supports SBIRT.
  • The grantee staff are highly experienced grant implementers.
  • Policy steering committee (PSC) membership includes broad essential stakeholder representation.

Challenges

  • Ohio has both a State and county system of government. The role of the county government in broad SBIRT dissemination and adoption is not well defined.
  • Ohio is a large and complex system spanning a large geographical area.

Implementation Plan

Strengths

  • The grantee has engaged essential stakeholders in this project.
  • The grantee has good training capacity.
  • The grantee has implemented SBIRT services on time using a staged implementation process.
  • The grantee effectively uses evaluation data to monitor and support project implementation.
  • Practice sites have clearly determined workflow and use a team-based approach.
  • SBIRT activities are being integrated into a tablet application that can be uploaded into electronic health records (EHRs).
  • All primary care sites have EHRs that can work within the State’s HIE.

Challenges

  • There is limited community-based substance abuse treatment capacity, with few Spanish-speaking clinicians and long wait-lists at certain facilities. This will make it challenging for the grantee to meet its performance expectation of clients referred for treatment being seen within 1 week or less.
  • The loss of two community primary care providers scheduled to implement SBIRT services in year 1 creates challenges for meeting performance targets.
  • Community behavioral health providers appear to have a range of challenges with readiness and capacity related to using EHRs and participating in the State’s HIE.

Community Linkages, Partners, and Participation

Strengths

  • The State is planning future SBIRT implementation within the Ohio Army National Guard.
  • Partnering with Kettering Health Systems leverages existing in-State SBIRT capacity.
  • Wright State University provides statewide telehealth as a safety net treatment response.

Challenges

  • There appears to be limited integration of the State’s community primary care with behavioral health providers.

Client Outreach, Recruitment, and Referral

Strengths

  • The grantee is delivering population-based screening for substance use risks and depression.

Challenges

  • Accessing specialty treatment has been described as challenging because of limited provider capacity and wait-lists for services.
  • The grantee is not providing embedded brief treatment at all practice sites.

Affordable Care Act Readiness

Strengths

The grantee has initiated strategies associated with the Affordable Care Act implementation:

  • Use of EHRs in all practice settings that are capable of participation in the State’s HIE
  • Embedding SBIRT procedures into primary care sites’ EHRs
  • Engaging essential stakeholder/policymakers on the PSC to address issues related to the Affordable Care Act

Challenges

  • The current capacity and readiness of community behavioral health systems has been identified as a challenge.

Sustainability Planning

Strengths

  • The PSC will play an important role in supporting long-term sustainability.
  • Medicaid and Medicare codes for screening and brief intervention are active in Ohio.

Challenges

  • The active Medicaid codes limit which providers can deliver services and under what circumstances.
  • In support of long-term sustainability, practice sites will need to develop viable business practice models for SBIRT within diverse practice settings.

Grantee Evaluation

Strengths

  • The grantee has contracted with an experienced evaluator to provide comprehensive evaluation services, including formative evaluation in support of implementation and Government Performance and Results Act (GPRA) baseline and followup activities.
  • The grantee uses a sophisticated Web-based data reporting system created by the grantee.

Challenges

  • The grantee has considered using behavioral health staff to conduct GPRA followup.

Recommended Enhancements

GRANTEE-LED ENHANCEMENTS

  • Program Vision and Design
    • Consider enhancing collaboration with county behavioral authorities and community providers to support behavioral health and primary care collaboration and integration.
  • Grantee Leadership
    • Consider engaging with representatives from the county provider association to clarify the role of county systems in support of SBIRT dissemination. Once roles are clarified, consider allocating resources to the county provider association to support these efforts.
    • Consider expanding staffing to support effective practice site communication and implementation. Consider funding this position through the primary care association as other grantees have done successfully.
  • Implementation Plan
    • Consider working with community alcohol and other drug providers to improve access to treatment.
    • Consider memorandums of understanding (MOUs) between primary care providers and community behavioral health as a required part of the upcoming Request for Proposals for primary care providers.
    • Communicate proactively with the Government Project Officer to describe plans to address the shortfall in first-year performance targets.
    • Confirm a baseline understanding of community provider readiness and capacity for EHR and HIE participation.
  • Community Linkages, Partners, and Participation
    • Develop MOUs, Business Associate Agreements, and Qualified Service Organizational Agreements between primary care and behavioral health providers to help improve treatment referrals and coordination of care.
  • Client Outreach, Recruitment, and Referral
    • Strengthen relationships with community treatment providers.
    • Explore options for improving access to timely substance abuse treatment services.
  • Affordable Care Act Readiness
    • Consider engaging and collaborating with county and community behavioral health systems to build capacity and support behavioral health and primary care integration.
  • Sustainability Planning
    • Consider working with the PSC to modify current Medicaid language regarding SBIRT.
    • Consider working with State provider associations to discuss, plan, and support developing viable business practice models.
  • Grantee Evaluation
    • Consider dedicating time of other staff, such as the care coordinators, to conduct GPRA followup.

POTENTIAL TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE REQUESTS

  • Implementation Plan
    • Consider building internal capacity for brief treatment to be provided within primary care sites.
    • Develop a HIT plan to support the State’s efforts toward HIT integration at the community, county, and State levels and help guide allocation of resources. (The grantee has requested a HIT plan template to help inform this planning process.)
  • Client Outreach, Recruitment, and Referral
    • After basic SBIRT implementation, consider moving forward with developing brief treatment capacity at primary care sites.
  • Affordable Care Act Readiness
    • Complete a strategic plan for HIT to help guide the alignment of EHR/HIT systems and inform decisions for the allocation of resources.
  • Sustainability Planning
    • Work with State provider associations to initiate discussion and planning in support of developing viable business practice models.
