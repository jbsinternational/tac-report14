New Mexico

  • Program Information
  • Site Visit Report

Dates:
May 20–22, 2014

Site Visit Team Members:
Contracting Staff: Joseph Hyde, M.A., LMHC; Beth Johnson, M.P.H.

Sites Visited:
First Nations Community Healthsource
Jemez Pueblo Health Clinic
Sangre de Cristo Community Health Partnership

Program Vision and Design

Strengths

  • New Mexico strongly endorses an integrated approach to substance use and views SBIRT as an essential set of services within primary care.
  • The State endorses and supports behavioral health and primary care integration.
  • The State is committed to reducing health disparities and prioritizes services to underserved populations.

Challenges

  • The model for behavioral health and primary care integration is evolving.
  • The State is at an important time in systems change for the payment systems of behavioral health care.

Grantee Leadership

Strengths

  • The SBIRT initiative receives high-level leadership support within the State and within payer systems.
  • The project team members offer depth of expertise and experience in training and supervision, evaluation, and implementation. They have full understanding of the clinical, systemic, and cultural issues related to SBIRT and demonstrate much enthusiasm.
  • The policy steering committee is composed of leaders who are well positioned to support SBIRT deployment and sustainability.
  • Team members are sensitive to the complex cultural, geographic, and economic challenges in New Mexico.

Challenges

  • The SBIRT project director is new to her role, managing a complex initiative that includes both service delivery and systems change.
  • Grant management is shared among the single State agency (SSA), the University of New Mexico, and the Sangre de Cristo Community Health Partnership.

Implementation Plan

Strengths

  • New Mexico SBIRT has been implemented in two sites serving Native Americans and Hispanics adults.
  • Practice site staff demonstrate strong buy-in for SBIRT services.
  • The implementation approach demonstrates a high level of sensitivity to the culture of the populations served.
  • The grantee is using telehealth for supervision and coaching to support implementation.
  • The project team is highly sensitive to comorbidities commonly associated with substance misuse and is implementing screening processes and evidence-based interventions. This presents both opportunities for more comprehensive care and some challenges discussed below.

Challenges

  • The grantee is significantly behind on its performance targets.
  • The implementation model is based largely on training and deployment of grant-funded peer support workers and behavioral health counselors.
  • SBIRT staff and services seem more colocated than integrated. Both sites have permanent behavioral health staff who currently have limited involvement in delivery of SBIRT services.
  • The New Mexico model calls for integrated treatment of comorbid mental health issues with evidence-based interventions including Seeking Safety, Community Reinforcement Approach, and IMPACT. This approach is an innovative and ambitious but also presents clinical, grant-specific, and operational challenges, particularly in light of current startup problems and meeting performance targets.

Community Linkages, Partners, and Participation

Strengths

  • The grantee has engaged with experienced and committed partners.

Challenges

  • Wait lists for higher levels of care are a concern.

Affordable Care Act Readiness

Strengths

  • Behavioral health and primary care integration is a State priority.
  • The State has adopted innovative use of telehealth technology for clinical services including clinical supervision.

Challenges

  • Specialty substance abuse treatment provider capacity spans a wide range (including readiness to adopt new business and clinical practices and electronic health records).

Sustainability Planning

Strengths

  • SBIRT is identified as an integrated part of the State Medicaid menu of services.
  • The policy steering committee includes high-level stakeholders who appear motivated to support sustainability.

Challenges

  • SBIRT is identified as included in the State Medicaid plan and Medicaid expansion; however, codes, rates, and policies, including access and credentialing, have yet to be fully confirmed.

Grantee Evaluation

Strengths

  • The State has engaged a highly experienced evaluation team with excellent knowledge of the culture and population served.

Challenges

  • The evaluation team can play an important role in providing the SBIRT management team with actionable performance data to monitor and support implementation and meet performance targets.

Recommended Enhancements

GRANTEE-LED ENHANCEMENTS

  • Grantee Leadership
    • Clarify the new permanent project director’s roles within the team, and delineate the communication and decisionmaking processes.
  • Implementation Plan
    • Develop a defined plan to expand service capacity to meet agreed-upon performance targets. Consider narrowing focus in next year to core SBIRT services.
    • Consider strategies to deploy SBIRT on a larger scale; for example, work through organizations that represent multiple clinical agencies such as New Mexico’s Primary Care Association or well-organized tribal collaboratives.
    • Consider augmenting the implementation model with training for regular clinical staff. Existing behavioral health providers could be trained to provide more intensive SBIRT services and improve access through strategies such as open-access scheduling and brief appointments compatible with primary care.
    • Consider training medical staff to provide brief interventions for mild-to-moderate-risk patients in a manner that fits with workflow.
  • Community Linkages, Partners, and Participation
    • Engage provider organizations such as the State provider association in support of broader dissemination and champion recruitment.
  • Client Outreach, Recruitment, and Referral
    • Have practice sites consider entering into Qualified Service Organization Agreements or Business Associate Agreements with specialty providers.
  • Affordable Care Act Readiness
    • Conduct a readiness assessment of specialty substance abuse treatment providers.
  • Sustainability Planning
    • Have the SSA actively involved in negotiations to ensure SBIRT rollout within Medicaid can support viable clinical and business practice.
  • Grantee Evaluation
    • Have the evaluation team work closely with the management team in developing plans to meet performance targets.
    • Have the evaluation team closely monitor the intervention’s front end for numbers of prescreens and full screens compared to number of patients served during the same time period. Some evaluation approaches involve collecting these data on a daily or weekly interval.
    • Consider discussing performance evaluation with other State project directors and evaluation teams.
