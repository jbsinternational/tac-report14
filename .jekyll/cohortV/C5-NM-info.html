Partnering Organizations

  • New Mexico Behavioral Health Services Division
  • Sangre de Cristo Community Health Partnership
  • The University of New Mexico (UNM) Center for Rural and Community Behavioral Health
  • UNM Center on Alcoholism, Substance Abuse, and Addictions

Goals

  • Expand and enhance the State and tribal continuum of care for substance misuse services.
  • Reduce substance use and increase quality of life statewide.
  • Reduce costly health care utilization.
  • Expand workforce development.
  • Enhance health information technology (HIT).
  • Ensure sustainability of SBIRT services.

Implementation Sites

  • UNM Hospital Trauma Center
  • Gallup Indian Health Services Medical Center
  • First Choice Community Health Care
  • First Nations Community Healthsource
  • Five Sandoval Indian Pueblos

Population Served

  • The prevalence of alcohol or drug dependence for most age groups in New Mexico is considerably higher than the national average (6.5 percent in New Mexico compared to 4.8 percent nationally).
  • The profile for the population of focus is similar to that of the overall State population in race, ethnicity, language, federally recognized tribe status, gender, age, socioeconomic characteristics, sexual identity (sexual orientation and gender identity), and other relevant factors.
  • New Mexico is the most ethnically diverse State in the continental United States (43 percent White, 44 percent Hispanic, 10 percent Native American, and 3 percent other). Spanish is spoken in 28.7 percent of homes statewide, compared to 11 percent nationally, and Native American languages are spoken in 5.8 percent of homes statewide.
  • There are 22 federally recognized tribes in New Mexico—19 Pueblos, 2 Apache reservations, and part of the Navajo Nation.
  • The population of focus is adults aged 18 years and older who receive treatment in primary care and community health settings and who are at risk for, or diagnosed with, a substance use disorder, with a priority population of Native Americans and veterans.
  • Those most in need of services are racially and ethnically diverse populations and individuals who are homeless, living in rural settings, experiencing multiple disorders, transitioning from one system to another, or incarcerated.
