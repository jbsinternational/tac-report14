Site Visits Timeline

Click on a site visit in the timeline below for information about the grantee and their site visit.

  • October 2010

    • University of Maryland

      October 13–15, 2010

      The Maryland M.D.s Making a Difference (MD3) program completed multiple activities in year 1 of its 5-year grant: developed a core training curriculum to be applied across residency program areas; finalized training curricula and didactic lectures, including materials and exercises, for the obstetrics-gynecology, psychiatry, and family medicine residency program areas and started training for those program areas; designed faculty needs assessment to gather information from each residency program regarding how they address substance abuse and behavior change and their scheduling of didactics; finalized a local evaluation protocol; finalized tools for measuring resident knowledge, attitude, and behavior change; and finalized a screening module to be integrated into the EPIC electronic medical record system.

  • December 2010

    • University of Missouri

      December 6–7, 2010

      The University of Missouri (MU) SBIRT medical residency program completed several activities in year 1 of its 5-year grant: developed a core training curriculum to be applied across residency program areas; commenced training for the family medicine, internal medicine, and psychiatry residency programs; adapted the training for surgery; started training nursing students and third-year medical students in the psychiatry block; started training nonresident faculty; developed evaluation instruments; and finalized a tool to measure change in resident attitude, knowledge, and skill.

  • January - February 2011

    • Albany Medical Center

      January 18–19, 2011

      In its first 3 grant years, the Albany Medical Center SBIRT medical residency program developed a comprehensive online training; specialty-specific training programs, including demonstrations, videos, role plays, resident information packets, mock scripts, and articles; specialty-specific standardized patients and patient scenarios; screening tools, evaluation tools, pocket cards, and a resource guide. The program also launched an SBIRT Web site; trained approximately 200 residents and 15 faculty from the psychiatry, internal medicine, pediatrics, family medicine, obstetrics and gynecology, and emergency medicine specialty areas; trained psychology postdoctoral students and interns; implemented SBIRT fully in an outpatient psychiatry clinic; and submitted a manuscript to the Journal of Academic Medicine.

    • Boston Children’s Hospital

      January 31–February 1, 2011

      In its first 3 years of grant funding, the Boston Children’s Hospital (BCH) SBIRT medical residency program completed multiple activities: developed a core training curriculum focused on pediatrics; commenced training for the adolescent medicine, developmental medicine, children’s emergency medicine, and child psychiatry residency program areas; developed and used SBIRT algorithms for each training area; developed a Web-based assessment tool for the Emergency Department; created and distributed adolescent-targeted substance abuse education material; and developed evaluation instruments.

    • University of Pittsburgh School of Pharmacy

      February 16–17, 2011

      In its first 3 grant years, the university’s SBIRT Medical and Residency Training (SMaRT) program developed, evaluated, and refined a core curriculum applicable across specialties. The program also developed curriculum components for specific populations and primary care settings and for faculty and advanced learners; started training for four medical specialties—family medicine, general internal medicine, obstetrics/gynecology, and pediatrics; acquired continuing medical education credits for community preceptors; developed curricular tools for champions, faculty, residents, and guests; developed protocols for training, implementation, testing, evaluation, and clinical application; developed papers, abstracts, posters, and a resource guide; developed evaluation tools and checklists; launched the “Get SMaRT” Web site; and initiated a Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System pilot study.

  • April 2011

    • University of California, San Francisco

      April 7–8, 2011

      The University of California, San Francisco (UCSF), SBIRT medical residency program completed several activities during the first 18 months of its 5-year grant: developed a faculty train-the-trainer curriculum and adapted general preceptor training; developed a comprehensive, interactive 10-hour training for primary care residents; completed SBIRT training by the first cohort of primary care residents; focused on system changes within clinics as the core component of SBIRT implementation; developed and used three standardized patients; used standardized patients for pre- and post-assessments; developed culturally and linguistically appropriate screening tools tailored to the diverse San Francisco population; disseminated training materials; used pain contracts for managing patients who use opioids regularly; and established an SBIRT requirement for preceptor training.

  • May 2011

    • ACCESS Community Health Network Medical Center

      May 10–11, 2011

      In its first 2½ years of grant funding, ACCESS developed four Web-based modules for SBIRT training; provided staff support for national efforts to create a core curriculum for SBIRT; trained new physicians and medical professionals, including National Health Service Corps professionals; submitted an abstract that was accepted as a poster presentation at the American Public Health Association national conference; and submitted an article on curriculum development to a peer-reviewed journal. Plans for the third year included completing and implementing the e-learning modules, expanding the evaluation of SBIRT, increasing resident training time at current SBIRT sites, and expanding SBIRT training among additional affiliated federally qualified health centers.

    • Baylor College of Medicine

      May 12–13, 2011

      In its first 2 grant years, the Baylor College of Medicine (BCM) InSight SBIRT Medical Residency Training Program developed a core curriculum applicable across specialties plus an expanded curriculum for faculty champions and resident champions. The program also provided level 1 SBIRT training to all 2011 first-year residents in family medicine, internal medicine, psychiatry, and internal medicine-pediatrics; recruited and trained faculty champions and resident champions; commenced training for emergency medicine; implemented learning experiences and champion precepting for residents; developed many curricular tools; developed a training program Web site; incorporated screening tools into Baylor Clinic’s electronic medical record system; implemented a self-perpetuating train-the-trainer model; and commenced a project process evaluation.

  • July 2013

    • San Francisco General Hospital

      July 14–15, 2011

      In its first 3 grant years, the San Francisco General Hospital (SFGH) SBIRT medical residency program developed a comprehensive curriculum adaptable to clinical settings and diverse populations; delivered trainings to the SFGH Department of Medicine Primary Care Internal Medicine residency training program, the Health Equity: Academics and Advocacy Training (HEAAT) track, and the Family and Community Medicine (FCM) residency training program; began implementing SBIRT into pediatric, obstetrics and gynecology, and surgery residencies; and partnered with the Behavioral Health Access Clinic (BHAC) to foster referrals to treatment and resident participation in clinic experiential learning. SFGH was continuing to identify and mentor residents, students, and faculty to champion SBIRT among their peers, faculty, and preceptors.

    • Mercer University

      July 26–27, 2011

      In its first 2 grant years, the Mercer Medical Residency Training Program developed a comprehensive, interactive 12-module training for primary care residents; completed SBIRT training by two cohorts of primary care residents; integrated SBIRT within clinic settings, including standardized protocols involving residents and allied staff; integrated SBIRT into the electronic medical record system; developed and used standardized patients; and developed a training and protocol to address pain management and prescription opioid misuse, abuse, and dependence. The protocol is an adaptation of SBIRT to address the patient population’s unique needs. Staff reported plans to submit a proposal to the National Institute on Drug Abuse to further study the protocol’s efficacy in a primary care setting.

  • August 2013

    • University of Texas Health Science Center at San Antonio

      August 17–18, 2011

      In its first 3 grant years, the University of Texas Health Science Center at San Antonio (UTHSCSA) SBIRT-South Texas Area Residency Training (S-START) program received the support of key “change” leaders; created a Web site that includes modules, announcements, and general information; embedded screening questions into the family and community medicine electronic medical record system; incorporated screening questions and cueing into paper-based outpatient medical record forms; and developed pocket cards to remind residents to use SBIRT at each patient encounter and to assess the SBIRT protocol’s fidelity. Pediatrics planned to launch a project for residents, involving iPads preloaded with SBIRT references, including the resource directory.

    • Natividad Medical Center

      August 24‒25, 2011

      In its first 3 grant years, Natividad developed an interactive 16-hour training for family medicine residents; integrated SBIRT into clinic settings’ routine workflows; integrated SBIRT into outreach settings for marginalized populations, including the homeless, migrant farm laborers, at-risk adolescents, veterans, and individuals in recovery; required all residents to complete a community medicine project; integrated processes and documentation for SBIRT into the electronic medical record system; and trained community health outreach workers (promotores) to incorporate SBIRT into outreach for migrant farm laborers. The program expects to increase collaboration with the county health department and to adopt a patent-centered medical home model for the Laurel Family Practice Clinic.

    • Indiana University Medical Center

      August 31–September 1, 2011

      Toward the end of year 2 of its 5-year grant, the Indiana University School of Medicine (IUSM) SBIRT medical residency program had completed several activities: developed Web modules for social workers, nurses, and medical nutritionists; networked with organizations interested in motivational interviewing (MI) training; fostered a strong interest in SBIRT and MI among clinic staff, including Primary Care Center staff; developed and nurtured a strong SBIRT dissemination model; included an external organization to develop curriculum materials; and developed marketing materials and branding, such as “We Ask Everyone” posters strategically placed in every clinic.

  • September 2013

    • Kettering Medical Center

      September 14–15, 2011

      In the first 3 years of its 5-year grant, the Kettering Medical Center (KMC) SBIRT program completed several activities: created a Web site within the Kettering Medical Education Department that provides an overview of SBIRT with a brochure, SBIRT pocket cards, videos, online links to resources, and a Primary Care Reports article published in the Primary Care Physicians Journalin January 2011; developed creative computer games to reinforce SBIRT principles, including motivational interviewing; and sought approval to add SBIRT questions to the EPIC electronic medical record.

    • Yale University School of Medicine

      September 26‒27, 2011

      By year 3 of its 5-year grant, the Yale University School of Medicine Medical Residency Training Program had achieved many accomplishments: exceeded training goals; developed, implemented, and evaluated a core curriculum in each specialty area; developed grant-related products such as papers, abstracts, and presentations; developed evaluation tools and checklists to assess residents’ proficiency in SBIRT; developed and launched a Web site (www.yale.edu/sbirt) with videos and other training resources; developed training tools, including laminated pocket cards and drinking and drug use agreement forms; and developed a demonstration version of the virtual coach tool to sustain the SBIRT training program after the grant ends.

    • Howard University

      September 29–30, 2011

      The Howard University (HU) SBIRT Medical Residency Program completed several activities as of the site visit date: created a manual of standard operating procedures that documents SBIRT background information, procedures, and requirements; implemented a cutting-edge standardized patient training facility where the residents perform SBIRT clinical skills examinations, receive written documentation of their performance, view a video recording of their patient encounter, and receive immediate feedback from the standardized patient; and developed an integrated curriculum that includes both didactic and experiential learning components (including lecture, practice session, online modules, five documented patient encounters, and a clinical skills examination).

  • October 2013

    • Oregon Health and Science University

      October 20–21, 2011

      In its first 3 grant years, the Oregon Health and Science University (OHSU) School of Medicine medical residency training program developed a core curriculum using the brief negotiated interview model that has been implemented in the family medicine, internal medicine, and preventive medicine residency programs; developed evaluation tools to assess SBIRT training and clinic implementation; developed laminated pocket cards, role plays, billing guides, and other training tools; launched a Web site (www.sbirtoregon.org) featuring the full curriculum, training videos, screening instruments, and resources; adopted a systems approach to SBIRT training; built capacity within practice sites before having residents begin practicing SBIRT; and organized its practice sites as patient-centered medical homes.
