Goals

  • Develop and implement training programs to teach medical residents skills to provide evidence-based SBIRT in a culturally competent manner for patients who either have or are at risk for a substance use disorder.
  • Promote adoption of SBIRT through delivery of training to local and statewide medical communities for wider dissemination of SBIRT practices.

Population Served

  • In 2006, the Medical Center of Central Georgia, Mercer's primary teaching hospital system, identified more than 10,000 inpatient and outpatient alcohol and drug-related visits by 3,632 different patients.
  • Survey data suggest that approximately 200,000 persons in Bibb County are at-risk alcohol or substance users who could potentially benefit from screening and brief intervention.
  • In 2008, approximately 7.7 percent of North Carolinians aged 12 and older report past-month illicit drug use, and 19 percent report past-month binge alcohol use. Main drugs of abuse include crack cocaine, marijuana, and prescription drugs. Methamphetamine use is increasing, particularly in the western counties near Wake Forest University.
  • In 2006, North Carolina had more than 16,000 admissions to drug and alcohol treatment programs. This number pales in comparison to the more than half-million citizens who, in 2008, reported needing but not receiving treatment for substance abuse within the past year.
  • The South Carolina Department of Alcohol and Other Drug Abuse Services (DAODAS) estimates that approximately 236,000 out of 4.3 million South Carolina residents (5.5 percent) have "substance abuse problems that require immediate intervention and treatment." In 2006–2007, DAODAS served 48,299 clients (20 percent of those needing treatment), so the unmet need for screening, diagnosis, and intervention remains high.
